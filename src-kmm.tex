In this section, we shift our focus to a more quantitative understanding of topological insulators, following our qualitative presentation in section \ref{sec:topins}.
For this purpose, we study a specific model of a two-dimensional topological insulator introduced by Kane and Mele.
They described a two-dimensional topological insulator model with Cartan label AII, along with a formula to compute the corresponding topological invariant.
Our goal is to frame their paper \cite{04d6b664} within the context of section \ref{sec:topins}, enhancing our understanding of topological insulators through a more detailed example.
Consequently, we will first describe the Kane-Mele model as an insulator comprising a crystal structure, a Hamiltonian, and its direct integral decomposition, followed by a discussion of the topological invariant.
By exploring a more fundamental level than before, we can relate it to the IQHE and identify it as the \textit{quantum spin Hall effect (QSHE)}, named for its origin in the electron spin.
\\
First, let us examine the crystal structure of the Kane-Mele model.
It features a {\glqq}honeycomb{\grqq} structure.\footnote{You can draw it if you need convincing.} Formally, let $d \in (0,\infty)$ and
\begin{align*}
  d_{1}
  :=
  d
  \left(
    \frac{1}{2},
    -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
  \right)
  \in
  \mathbb{R}^{2}
  &&
  d_{2}
  :=
  d
  \left(
    \frac{1}{2},
    \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
  \right)
  \in
  \mathbb{R}^{2}
  &&
  d_{3}
  :=
  -
  (d_{1} + d_{2})
\end{align*}
Furthermore, let
\begin{align*}
  l_{1}
  :=
  d_{2}
  -
  d_{3}
  &&
  l_{2}
  :=
  d_{3}
  -
  d_{1}
  &&
  l_{3}
  :=
  -
  (l_{1}+l_{2})
\end{align*}
Then $L_{\textrm{km}} := \mathrm{span}_{\mathbb{Z}}(l_{1},l_{2})$ represents the lattice of the Kane-Mele model.
Next, for the c-basis, we choose $\mathfrak{C}_{\textrm{km}} := \lbrace 0,d_{2} \rbrace$ and $\mathsf{A}_{\textrm{km}} := \lbrace A,B \rbrace$ together with the function $f_{\textrm{km}} := \lbrace (0,A),(d_{2},B) \rbrace \subset \mathfrak{C}_{\textrm{km}} \times \mathsf{A}_{\textrm{km}}$.
The specific type of atomic nuclei is irrelevant to our theoretical considerations.
\\
Having specified the crystal structure of the Kane-Mele model, we now need the Hamilton operator that describes the underlying physics.
Since the Hamilton operator $H = H_{0} + M_{V}$ from section \ref{sec:topins} (where $H_{0}$ involves Pauli matrices) is too complicated, we employ a {\glqq}tight-binding approximation{\grqq} alongside several correction terms, making it sufficiently close to the original concept.
Descriptively, tight binding means that the electrons are tightly bound to a specific crystal site and therefore do not feel the potential generated by the other sites.
The use of this approximation is physically justified by the observation that the lattice framework from section \ref{sec:topins} is generally only valid for temperatures near absolute zero, meaning the electrons occupy the lowest energy levels.
In other words, they are probably close to an atomic nucleus.
Thus, in the tight-binding approximation, the crystal sites are assumed to be isolated quantum systems, with one Hamilton operator per crystal site/atomic nucleus.
As usual, an atomic nucleus is modeled as a Coulomb-like system.
Then, after solving the Schrödinger equation for these Coulomb-like systems (possibly subjected to a magnetic field), we obtain annihilation operators
\begin{align*}
  c_{(l,c)}
  &=
  (c_{(l,c)}^{\uparrow},c_{(l.c)}^{\downarrow})
\end{align*}
with two components due to the spin-$1/2$ nature of the electrons at every crystal site \begin{align*}
  (l,c)
  \in
  L_{\textrm{km}}
  \times
  \mathfrak{C}_{\textrm{km}}
  &=:
  \mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}}
\end{align*}
Being an \textit{annihilation operator} means that for a given eigenvector $\psi_{n}$ of the Hamilton operator (note the pure point spectrum) corresponding to the eigenvalue $E_{n}$ where $n \in \mathbb{N}$, the vector $c_{(l,c)}(\psi_{n})$ is an eigenvector for the eigenvalue $E_{n-1}$ if $n > 0$, and $0$ otherwise.
The adjoint $c_{(l,c)}^{\ast}$ of $c_{(l,c)}$ is called the \textit{creation operator} and behaves in the opposite manner.
In \textit{second quantization}, tight binding corresponds to \textit{nearest-neighbor hopping}.
Two crystal sites are considered \textit{nearest neighbors} if their distance is $\Vert d_{2} \Vert$.
Nearest-neighbor hopping means that the annihilation of an electron at one site results in the creation of an electron at a nearest-neighbor site.
Let $\textrm{nn}_{(l,c)} \subset \mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}}$ be the set of nearest neighbors to the crystal site $(l,c) \in \mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}}$.
The tight-binding Hamilton operator is then given by
\begin{align*}
  H_{\textrm{nn}}
  &:=
  \lambda_{\textrm{nn}}
  \sum_{\langle i,j \rangle}
  c_{j}^{\ast}
  c_{i}
  :=
  \lambda_{\textrm{nn}}
  \sum_{i \in \mathbf{C}_{\textrm{km}}}
  \sum_{j \in \textrm{nn}_{i}}
  \langle
    c_{j}^{\ast},c_{i}
  \rangle
\end{align*}
for $\lambda_{\textrm{nn}} \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\langle \cdot,\cdot \rangle$ being the canonical dual pairing.
We introduce three corrections to this model, $H_{\textrm{nn}}$, to obtain the full Kane-Mele model, $H_{\textrm{km}}$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(SE)]
  The first correction to the tight-binding approximation in the Kane-Mele model is the self-energy term.
  Its physical origin stems from the fact that two adjacent sites are not a priori equivalent.
  In second quantization, we obtain
\begin{align*}
  H_{\textrm{se}}
  &:=
  \lambda_{\textrm{se}}
  \sum_{l \in L_{\textrm{km}}}
  \left(
    c_{(l,0)}^{\ast}
    c_{(l,0)}
    -
    c_{(l,d_{2})}^{\ast}
    c_{(l,d_{2})}
  \right)
\end{align*}
for $\lambda_{\textrm{se}} \in \mathbb{R}$.
\item[(SO)]
  The second correction term is spin-orbit coupling, which originates from the interaction of the electron spin with the magnetic field generated by the electron's momentum and the electric field of the atomic nuclei.
  First, note that two crystal sites are \textit{next-nearest neighbors} if their distance is $\Vert l_{1} \Vert$.
  Let $\textrm{nnn}_{(l,c)} \subset \mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}}$ be the set of next-nearest neighbors to the crystal site $(l,c) \in \mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}}$.
  Then the spin-orbit term is:
\begin{align*}
  H_{\textrm{so}}
  &:=
  \mathrm{i}
  \lambda_{\textrm{so}}
  \sum_{\langle\langle i,j \rangle\rangle}
  \nu_{ij}
  c_{j}^{\ast}
  \sigma_{3}
  c_{i}
  :=
  \mathrm{i}
  \lambda_{\textrm{so}}
  \sum_{i \in \mathbf{C}_{\textrm{km}}}
  \sum_{j \in \textrm{nnn}_{i}}
  \nu_{ij}
  c_{j}^{\ast}
  \sigma_{3}
  c_{i}
\end{align*}
for $\lambda_{\textrm{so}} \in \mathbb{R}$ and
\begin{align*}
  \nu_{ij}
  &:=
  \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}\Vert d_{2} \Vert^{2}}
  \left(
    (D_{i} \vert e_{1})
    (D_{j} \vert e_{2})
    -
    (D_{i} \vert e_{2})
    (D_{j} \vert e_{1})
  \right)
  =
  \pm
  1
\end{align*}
where $D_{i},D_{j} \in \lbrace \pm d_{1},\pm d_{2},\pm d_{3} \rbrace$ such that $D_{i} + D_{j}$ is the vector connecting next-nearest neighbor crystal sites $i$ and $j$.
\item[(RA)]
The third and final correction in the Kane-Mele model is a Rashba term.
Its physical origin is an electric field in the $e_{3}$ direction.
This could be caused, for example, by a substrate supporting the two-dimensional layer, though external interference is also possible.
Here, spin-orbit coupling yields
\begin{align*}
  H_{\textrm{ra}}
  &:=
  \mathrm{i}
  \lambda_{\textrm{ra}}
  \sum_{\langle i,j \rangle}
  c_{j}^{\ast}
  ((D_{ij}\vert e_{2})\sigma_{1} - (D_{ij} \vert e_{1})\sigma_{2})
  c_{i}
\end{align*}
for $\lambda_{\textrm{ra}} \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\Vert d_{2} \Vert D_{ij} \in \lbrace \pm d_{1},\pm d_{2},\pm d_{3} \rbrace$ being the vector connecting nearest neighbor sites $i$ and $j$.
\end{enumerate}
Overall, the Kane-Mele Hamilton operator
\begin{align*}
  H_{\textrm{km}}
  &:=
  H_{\textrm{nn}}
  +
  H_{\textrm{se}}
  +
  H_{\textrm{so}}
  +
  H_{\textrm{ra}}
\end{align*}
approximates {\glqq}reality{\grqq} sufficiently well for our purposes.
Even further simplifying the operator so that its domain is restricted to $\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$ is sufficient.
The physical justification for this is that at absolute zero, the energy bands are fully occupied up to the valence band.
The \textbf{valence band} is the highest band below the Fermi level.
An additional electron then {\glqq}hops{\grqq} from the lowest available energy state above the Fermi level of one atomic nucleus to the lowest available state above the Fermi level of a nearest neighbor.
Therefore, the electronically interesting physics occurs near the Fermi level, and only one state per nucleus needs to be considered.
This restriction is reflected by the space $\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$, which also accounts for the possible lifting of spin degeneracy due to magnetic fields.
Working with states in $\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$ allows us to translate compositions of creation and annihilation operators into spatial translation operators.
Since nearest neighbors $i$ and $j$ are connected by a vector $\pm d_{k}$ for $k \in \{1,2,3\}$, we replace $c_{j}^{\ast}c_{i}$ with a translation operator $T_{\pm d_{k}}$ defined by
\begin{align*}
  T_{d_{k}}(\psi)(l,c)
  &:=
  \begin{cases}
    \psi(l - d_{k} + d_{2},0)
    &
    c
    =
    d_{2}
    \\
    0
    &
    \text{else}
  \end{cases}
\end{align*}
and $T_{-d_{k}} := T_{d_{k}}^{\ast}$.
The definition of $T_{l_{k}}$ follows naturally.
Using the same symbols for the Hamilton operators, we get
\begin{align*}
  H_{\textrm{nn}}
  &=
  \lambda_{\textrm{nn}}
  \sum_{k=1}^{3}
  \left(
    T_{d_{k}}
    +
    T_{-d_{k}}
  \right)
  \otimes
  1_{\mathbb{C}^{2}}
  \\
  H_{\textrm{se}}
  &=
  \lambda_{\textrm{se}}
  (\chi_{A} - \chi_{B})
  1_{\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C})}
  \otimes
  1_{\mathbb{C}^{2}}
  \\
  H_{\textrm{so}}
  &=
  -
  \mathrm{i}
  \lambda_{\textrm{so}}
  (\chi_{A} - \chi_{B})
  \sum_{k=1}^{3}
  \left(
    T_{l_{k}}
    -
    T_{-l_{k}}
  \right)
  \\
  H_{\textrm{ra}}
  &=
  \mathrm{i}
  \lambda_{\textrm{ra}}
  \left(
    \left(
      T_{d_{1}}
      -
      T_{-d_{1}}
    \right)
    \otimes
    \left(
      -
      \frac{\sqrt{3}\sigma_{1} + \sigma_{2}}{2}
    \right)
    +
    \left(
      T_{d_{2}}
      -
      T_{-d_{2}}
    \right)
    \otimes
    \left(
      \frac{\sqrt{3}\sigma_{1} + \sigma_{2}}{2}
    \right)
    +
    \left(
      T_{d_{3}}
      -
      T_{-d_{3}}
    \right)
    \otimes
    \sigma_{2}
  \right)
\end{align*}
where $\chi_{A}(\psi((l,c))) = 1$ if $(c,A) \in f_{\textrm{km}}$ and similarly for $\chi_{B}$.
This formulation of $H_{\textrm{km}}$ is the Hamiltonian we will work with going forward.
\\
With the Hamiltonian $H_{\textrm{km}}$ established, we are again interested in decomposing it according to crystal momentum, similar to section \ref{sec:topins}, to reveal topological information.
A modified Bloch-Floquet transform can be defined in much the same way as in section \ref{sec:topins}.
However, we will use the standard Bloch-Floquet transform here so that $\tilde{H}$ exhibits the periodicity of the reciprocal lattice.
The standard Bloch-Floquet transform is unitarily equivalent to the modified version, making them equally appropriate.
The advantage of the modified transform is that the domain of $\tilde{H}(k)$ does not depend on $k$, which is highly convenient for mathematical considerations.
Nevertheless, the standard \textbf{Bloch-Floquet transformation} on $\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$ is defined by
\begin{align*}
  \mathcal{B}
  &\colon
  \ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}})
  \times
  \mathbb{C}^{2}
  \to
  L_{2}
  \left(
    B_{L_{\textrm{km}}},
    \ell^{2}(\mathfrak{C}_{\textrm{km}}) 
    \times
    \mathbb{C}^{2}
  \right)
  ,\qquad
  f
  \mapsto
  (\mathcal{B}(f))(k)
  :=
  \sum_{l \in L_{\textrm{km}}}
  \exp(\mathrm{i}(k \vert l))
  f(\cdot - l)
\end{align*}
Notice that this depends on the choice of the c-basis.
This is the trade-off for the periodicity of $\tilde{H}$.
For the direct integral decomposition, we obtain
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{nn}}(k)
  &=
  \lambda_{\textrm{nn}}
  \left(
    \left(
      1
      +
      \cos((k \vert l_{1}))
      +
      \cos((k \vert l_{3}))
    \right)
    \sigma_{1}
    \otimes
    1_{\mathbb{C}^{2}}
    +
    \left(
      \sin((k \vert l_{1}))
      -
      \sin((k \vert l_{3}))
    \right)
    \sigma_{2}
    \otimes
    1_{\mathbb{C}^{2}}
  \right)
  \\
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{se}}(k)
  &=
  \lambda_{\textrm{se}}
  \sigma_{3}
  \otimes
  1_{\mathbb{C}^{2}}
  \\
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{so}}(k)
  &=
  -
  2
  \lambda_{\textrm{so}}
  \sum_{k=1}^{3}
  \sin((k \vert l_{k}))
  \sigma_{3}
  \otimes
  \sigma_{3}
  \\
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{ra}}(k)
  &=
  \lambda_{\textrm{ra}}
  \left(
    \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
    \left(
      1
      -
      \cos((k \vert l_{3}))
    \right)
    \sigma_{2}
    \otimes
    \sigma_{1}
    +
    \left(
      \cos((k \vert l_{1}))
      -
      \frac{1}{2}
      \left(
        1
        +
        \cos((k \vert l_{3}))
      \right)
    \right)
    \sigma_{2}
    \otimes
    \sigma_{2}
  \right.
  \\
  &\phantom{=}
  \left.
    -
    \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
    \sin((k \vert l_{3}))
    \sigma_{1}
    \otimes
    \sigma_{1}
    -
    \left(
      \sin((k \vert l_{1}))
      +
      \frac{1}{2}
      \sin((k \vert l_{3}))
    \right)
    \sigma_{1}
    \otimes
    \sigma_{2}
  \right)
\end{align*}
Due to the linearity of the Bloch-Floquet transformation,
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(k)
  &=
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{nn}}(k)
  +
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{se}}(k)
  +
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{so}}(k)
  +
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{ra}}(k)
\end{align*}  
holds.
Again, the family $\lbrace \tilde{P}_{\textrm{km}}^{I}(k) \colon k \in B_{L_{\textrm{km}}} \rbrace$ is identified with a complex vector bundle.
\\
We previously claimed that the Kane-Mele model possesses Cartan label AII.
Therefore, it should exhibit time-reversal symmetry but lack charge conjugation symmetry (or any combination of the two).
While we will not explicitly prove the absence of the latter, we will sketch the proof for the presence of the former.
To this end, note that on $\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$, a time-reversal operator derived from Wigner's theorem takes the form
\begin{align*}
  \Theta
  &:=
  \left(
    1_{\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C})}
    \otimes
    \sigma_{2}
  \right)
  \circ
  \kappa
\end{align*}
where $\kappa$ denotes complex conjugation.
It is easy to verify that applying $\Theta$ twice simply changes the sign.
Furthermore, $\Theta$ is clearly an antiunitary operator, as it is the composition of a unitary operator and complex conjugation.
Hence, for all $\psi,\hat{\psi} \in \ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$,
\begin{align*}
  (\Theta(\psi) \vert \Theta(\hat{\psi}))
  &=
(\hat{\psi} \vert \psi)\end{align*}
holds.
This immediately yields, for all $\psi,\hat{\psi} \in \ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$,
\begin{align*}
  (\Theta(\psi) \vert \hat{\psi})
  &=
  (\Theta(\hat{\psi}) \vert \Theta(\Theta(\psi)))
  =
  -
  (\Theta(\hat{\psi}) \vert \psi)
\end{align*}
from which we conclude for all $\psi \in \ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$
\begin{align*}
  (\Theta(\psi) \vert \psi)
  &=
  0
\end{align*}
by considering the case $\psi = \hat{\psi}$.
If we apply the Bloch-Floquet transform to $\Theta$ via $\mathcal{B}\Theta\mathcal{B}^{-1} =: \tilde{\Theta}$, then $\tilde{\Theta}$ is given by
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{\Theta}(\psi)(-k)
  &:=
  \left(
    \left(
      1_{\mathbb{C}^{2}}
      \otimes
      \sigma_{2}
    \right)
    \circ
    \kappa
  \right)
  (\psi(k))
\end{align*}
for $\psi \in  L_{2}(B_{L_{\textrm{km}}},\ell^{2}(\mathfrak{C}_{\textrm{km}}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2})$.
As before, applying $\tilde{\Theta}$ twice simply flips the sign.
Moreover, there is an equivalence
\begin{align*}
  \Theta
  \circ
  H_{\textrm{km}}
  \circ
  \Theta^{-1}
  =
  H_{\textrm{km}}
  \qquad
  &\Leftrightarrow
  \qquad
  \forall
  [k]
  \in
  \mathbb{R}^{2}/L_{\textrm{km}}^{\prime}
  \quad
  \tilde{\Theta}
  \circ
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(k)
  \circ
  \tilde{\Theta}^{-1}
  =
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(-k)
\end{align*}
The latter proposition can be verified explicitly using our definitions for $\Theta$; this equivalence thus proves that $H_{\textrm{km}}$ possesses time-reversal symmetry.
\\
With time-reversal symmetry established, we are now in a position to derive an easily calculable formula to determine the topological phase.
To this end, let $\psi_{0}(k)$ and $\psi_{1}(k)$ denote the eigenvectors corresponding to the lowest eigenvalues $E_{0}(k)$ and $E_{1}(k)$ of $\tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(k)$.
Furthermore, note that there exists $[k] \in \mathbb{R}^{2}/L_{\textrm{km}}^{\prime}$ such that
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(-k)
  &=
  \tilde{\Theta}
  \circ
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(k)
  \circ
  \tilde{\Theta}^{-1}
  =
  \tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(k)
\end{align*}
$[k] = [0]$ is one such example.
The points $[k] = [\frac{1}{2}l_{1,2}^{\prime}]$ and $[k] = [\frac{1}{2}(l_{1}^{\prime} + l_{2}^{\prime})]$ also share this property, which can be seen directly from the shape of $\tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}$ above and the periodicity of the sine and cosine functions.
We denote the subspace of these points by $\mathtt{Ev}$.
The subspace containing the points $[k]$ for which
\begin{align*}
  (\psi_{i}(k) \vert \tilde{\Theta}(\psi_{j})(-k))
  &=
  0
\end{align*}
for all $i,j = 0,1$ is denoted by $\mathtt{Odd}$.
Note that for $i = j$, this equality always holds due to the antiunitarity of $(1_{\mathbb{C}^{2}} \otimes \sigma_{2}) \circ \kappa$.
Consequently, $\psi_{i}(k)$ and $\tilde{\Theta}(\psi_{i})(k)$ are linearly independent.
For $[k] \in \mathtt{Ev}$, it is apparent that $\tilde{\Theta}(\psi_{0})(k)$ is also an eigenvector for the same eigenvalue as $\psi_{0}(k)$ of $\tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(k)$, since $\tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(k)$ commutes with $\tilde{\Theta}$.
Thus, for $[k] \in \mathtt{Ev}$, we can choose
\begin{align*}
  \psi_{1}(k)
  &=
  \tilde{\Theta}(\psi_{0})(k)
\end{align*}
Regardless, by defining
\begin{align*}
  \mathrm{pf}(k)
  &:=
  (\psi_{0}(k) \vert \tilde{\Theta}(\psi_{1})(k))
\end{align*}
it becomes clear that
\begin{align*}
  \vert
    \mathrm{pf}(k)
  \vert
  &=
  \begin{cases}
    1
    & 
    [k] \in \mathtt{Ev}
    \\
    0
    &
    [k] \in \mathtt{Odd}
  \end{cases}
\end{align*}
If $[k]$ is in $\mathtt{Odd}$, then so is $[-k]$.
The topological phase is determined by counting the number of pairs $([k],[-k]) \in \mathtt{Odd} \times \mathtt{Odd}$ modulo $2$, where an even number corresponds to the ordinary insulating phase.
This quantity can be calculated using a winding number:
\begin{align*}
  I_{\textrm{km}}
  &:=
  \left(
    \lim_{\delta \downarrow 0}
    \frac{1}{2\pi\mathrm{i}}
    \oint_{\gamma}
    \nabla_{k}
    \ln(\mathrm{pf} + \mathrm{i}\delta)
    \mathrm{d}k
  \right)
  \mod
  2
  \in
  K_{\mathbb{R}}^{-2}(\lbrace x_{0} \rbrace)
  =
  \mathbb{Z}_{2}
\end{align*}
where $\gamma = \gamma_{1} + \gamma_{2} + \gamma_{3}$ is the contour composed of
\begin{align*}
  \gamma_{1}(t)
  :=
  tl_{1}^{\prime}
  &&
  \gamma_{2}(t)
  :=
  \gamma_{1}(1)
  +
  tl_{2}^{\prime}
  &&
  \gamma_{3}(t)
  :=
  \gamma_{2}(1)
  +
  tl_{3}^{\prime}
\end{align*}
for $t \in [0,1]$ in each case.\footnote{$\delta > 0$ is needed since $\mathtt{Odd}$ can contain points of $\mathrm{im}(\gamma)$ if $\lambda_{\textrm{se}} = 0$}
This is the formula for the topological phase originally given by Kane and Mele in \cite{04d6b664}.
However, there are alternative formulas for this calculation.
The reader is referred to \cite{fa76af21}, \cite{f6db8935}, or \cite{6ab9663f} for further examples.
Naturally, there is also a more K-theoretic perspective on the Kane-Mele model.
However, because the structure is richer, the details are slightly more involved than with plain $K_{\mathbb{K}}$:
\\
\begin{rem}
\label{rem:krtheory}
The operator $\tilde{\Theta} \circ \tilde{\Theta}$ induces a topological involution on the total space of the vector bundle associated with the Kane-Mele model.
A \textbf{topological involution} $i_{X} \colon X \to X$ is a homeomorphism such that $i_{X} = i_{X}^{-1}$.
And $[k] \mapsto [-k]$ serves as such an involution on the base space $\mathbb{R}^{2}/L_{\textrm{km}}^{\prime}$.
This makes the bundle {\glqq}Real{\grqq}: A vector bundle $(E,B,p,\mathbb{C}^{n})$ is defined as \textbf{Real}\footnote{Not to be confused with standard real vector bundles $\mathbb{R}-\mathbf{VB}$.} if there exist topological involutions $i_{B}$ and $i_{E}$ such that
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(RB1)]
$i_{B} \circ p = p \circ i_{E}$
\item[(RB2)]
$i_{E} \vert p^{-1}(b) \colon p^{-1}(b) \to p^{-1}(i_{B}(b))$ is antilinear for each $b \in B$
\end{enumerate}
  There is a variant of $K_{\mathbb{C}}$ tailored for Real vector bundles, known as \textit{(twisted) KR-theory}.
This is the appropriate K-theoretic tool for distinguishing the topological phases of the Kane-Mele model.
Standard $K_{\mathbb{C}}$ is inadequate here because the Chern number always vanishes; for dimension $n = 2$, this can be shown directly in the presence of time-reversal symmetry (see \cite{d02f3620}).
However, KR-theory and the formula above are linked by an index theorem similar to the Atiyah-Singer index theorem.
Further details can be found in \cite{fa76af21}, for example.
\\
\phantom{proven}
\hfill
$\square$
\end{rem}
Finally, let us take a closer look at the model's parameters and their impact on the topological phase.
This discussion will reveal a previously hidden relationship with the Chern number.
So, which parameters lead to which phase?
We distinguish three cases:
\begin{enumerate}
\item
  For $\lambda_{\textrm{nn}} \neq 0$ and $\lambda_{\textrm{se}} = \lambda_{\textrm{so}} = \lambda_{\textrm{ra}} = 0$, the model simplifies to standard graphene - at least when $\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}},\mathbb{C}) \times \mathbb{C}^{2}$ is the chosen Hilbert space (see Figure \ref{fig:specgraph}).
\begin{figure}[H]
\includegraphics[scale=0.4]{graphics/specgraph.png}

\item
  For $\lambda_{\textrm{nn}} \neq 0$, $\lambda_{\textrm{so}} \neq 0$, and $\lambda_{\textrm{ra}} = 0$, we can distinguish between two topological phases: the ordinary insulator phase for $\lambda_{\textrm{se}} > 3\sqrt{3}\lambda_{\textrm{so}}$ (see Figure \ref{fig:specins}), and the so-called \textit{quantum spin Hall (QSH)} phase for $\lambda_{\textrm{se}} < 3\sqrt{3}\lambda_{\textrm{so}}$ (see Figure \ref{fig:specqsh}).
Note that when $\lambda_{\textrm{ra}} = 0$, the third component of the spin is conserved, and $H_{\textrm{km}}$ can be written as $H_{\textrm{km}}^{\uparrow} + H_{\textrm{km}}^{\downarrow}$.
This provides an independent Hamilton operator for each spin direction.
Associated with each is an integer, $n^{\uparrow}$ or $n^{\downarrow}$, representing the Chern number of $E_{P_{\textrm{km}}^{F}}$ for the corresponding part of the Hamilton operator, as discussed in section \ref{sec:topins}.
Although $n^{\uparrow} + n^{\downarrow} = 0$ must hold due to time-reversal symmetry, the quantity
\begin{align*}
  \nu
  &:=
  \frac{n^{\uparrow} - n^{\downarrow}}{2}
  \in
  \mathbb{Z}
\end{align*}
is not necessarily zero.
In fact, according to \cite{f6db8935}, we find
\begin{align*}
  I_{\textrm{km}}
  &=
  \nu
  \mod
  2
\end{align*}
In this scenario, conductance can occur.
Specifically, if $n^{\uparrow} \neq 0$, there is a conductance of spin-up electrons, which is perfectly counterbalanced by a conductance of spin-down electrons, preserving the zero net sum.
This spin conductance is quantized.
Thus, one can speak of a \textit{quantized spin Hall\footnote{Please note that the physical origin of this phenomenon is the effective magnetic field resulting from spin-orbit coupling.} conductance} characterized by $\nu$.

\label{fig:specqsh}
\end{figure}
\item
  In the previous cases, we assumed $\lambda_{\textrm{ra}} = 0$.
However, this is physically unrealistic; the unavoidably emerging Rashba term breaks spin conservation, rendering the integer $\nu$ meaningless.
Nevertheless, the invariant $I_{\textrm{km}}$ remains valid.
There exists a threshold $x < 2\sqrt{3}\lambda_{\textrm{so}}$ such that the QSH phase is retained for $0 \leq \lambda_{\textrm{ra}} \leq x$, provided it was already present for $\lambda_{\textrm{ra}} = 0$ (see Figure \ref{fig:specrash}).
If $x > 0$, a spin conductance still exists; however, it is no longer strictly quantized.

\label{fig:specrash}
\end{figure}
\end{enumerate}
The Kane-Mele model is quite explicit.
Indeed, it is explicit enough to be implemented computationally, as we have done at \href{https://github.com/shtsoft/topins/blob/master/code/kmm.m}{https://github.com/shtsoft/topins/blob/master/code/kmm.m} to generate the energy band visualizations above.
Another advantage of this numerical implementation is the ability to empirically explore propositions regarding the physics of the Kane-Mele model.
Let us demonstrate this with a concluding example focused on the thermodynamic pressure of the Kane-Mele model in an external magnetic field.
\\
\begin{exa}
\label{exa:tdpressure}
This example presents a conjecture regarding a classical approximation of the Kane-Mele model's thermodynamic pressure in an external magnetic field, based on numerical analysis.
\\
First, an external magnetic field in the $e_{3}$ direction is described by the magnetic vector potential
\begin{align*}
  A(y_{1},y_{2})
  &:=
  (By_{2},0)
\end{align*}
for $B \in \mathbb{R}$.
  This can be incorporated into the Kane-Mele model by substituting the standard translation operators with \textit{magnetic translations} - that is, translation operators equipped with a position-dependent \textit{phase}, $\exp(\mathrm{i}\phi_{i}(x))$.
To this end, let $(x,0) := ((x_{1},x_{2}),0) \in \mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}}$.
Then, $\gamma_{i}^{x}(t) = x +td_{i}$ for $t \in [0,1]$ and $i =1,2,3$ represents a path connecting nearest neighbors, and the $\phi_{i}$ is given by
\begin{align*}
  \phi_{i}(x)
  =
  \int_{\gamma_{i}^{x}}
  (By_{2},0)
  \mathrm{d}y
  &=
  B
  \int_{0}^{1}
  (x_{2}+t(d_{i} \vert e_{2}),0)
  d_{i}
  \mathrm{d}t
  \\
  &=
  B
  (d_{i} \vert e_{1})
  \int_{0}^{1}
  (x_{2}+t(d_{i} \vert e_{2}))
  \mathrm{d}t
  =
  B
  (d_{i} \vert e_{1})
  x_{2}
  +
  \frac{1}{2}
  B
  (d_{i} \vert e_{1})
  (d_{i} \vert e_{2})
\end{align*}
Substituting the explicit values for $d_{i}$ yields
\begin{align*}
  \phi_{1}(x)
  =
  \frac{dB}{2}
  x_{2}
  -
  \frac{\sqrt{3}d^{2}B}{8}
  &&
  \phi_{2}(x)
  =
  \frac{dB}{2}
  x_{2}
  +
  \frac{\sqrt{3}d^{2}B}{8}
  &&
  \phi_{3}(x)
  =
  -
  dBx_{2}
\end{align*}
and the magnetic translations $T_{d_{i}}^{B}$ are defined as
\begin{align*}
  T_{d_{i}}^{B}(\psi)(x)
  &:=
  \exp(\mathrm{i}\phi_{i}(x))
  T_{d_{i}}(\psi)(x)
\end{align*}
The numerical results from \href{https://github.com/shtsoft/topins/blob/master/code/kmm.m}{https://github.com/shtsoft/topins/blob/master/code/kmm.m} suggest the following:
\\
\begin{con}
\label{con:pressofkmm}
Let $B = B_{0} + \varepsilon b$ where $B_{0} \cdot \mathrm{vol}_{2}(\mathbb{R}^{2}/L_{\textrm{km}}) \in 2\pi\mathbb{Z}$, $b \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\varepsilon > 0$.
Furthermore, let $\beta \in (0,\infty)$, $\mu = 0$ and $(\chi_{m})_{m\in\mathbb{N}^{\times}}$ such that $\chi_{m} \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^{2},\mathbb{R})$ has support in $\Lambda_{m} \supset \mathrm{supp}(\chi_{m})$ with $\chi_{m} \vert \Lambda_{m-1} = 1$ if $\Lambda_{m} := [-m,m] \times [-m,m]$.
Then, the limit
\begin{align*}
  \lim_{\beta \to \infty}
  \beta^{-1}
  \lim_{m\to\infty}
  \frac{\varepsilon^{2}}{\Vert \chi_{m} \Vert_{L_{1}}}
  \mathrm{tr}_{\ell^{2}(\mathrm{C}_{\textrm{km}} \times \mathbb{C}^{2})}
  \left(
    \chi_{m}(\varepsilon x)
    \cdot
    \ln
    \left(
      1
      +
      \exp
      \left(
        -
        \beta
        \left(
          H_{\textrm{km}}^{B}
          -
          \mu
        \right)
      \right)
    \right)
  \right)
\end{align*}
exists and is approximated by
\begin{align*}
  \lim_{\beta \to \infty}
  \frac{1}{4 \cdot \mathrm{vol}_{2}(\mathbb{R}^{2}/L_{\textrm{km}}^{\prime})}
  \sum_{i=0}^{3}
  \int_{\mathbb{R}^{2}/L_{\textrm{km}}^{\prime}}
  \left(
    1
    +
    \varepsilon
    b
    \Omega_{(i)}
  \right)
  \ln
  \left(
    1
    +
    \exp
    \left(
      -
      \beta
      \left(
        h_{(i)}
        -
        \mu
      \right)
    \right)
  \right)
  \mathrm{d}k
  +
  \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^{2})
\end{align*}
for sufficiently small $\varepsilon$, where
\begin{align*}
  \Omega_{(i)}(k)
  &:=
  -
  \mathrm{i}
  \cdot
  \mathrm{tr}_{\mathbb{C}^{4}}
  \left(
    \tilde{P}_{\textrm{km}}^{\lbrace i \rbrace}(k)
    \left[
      \partial_{1}
      \tilde{P}_{\textrm{km}}^{\lbrace i \rbrace}(k),
      \partial_{2}
      \tilde{P}_{\textrm{km}}^{\lbrace i \rbrace}(k)
    \right]
  \right)
  \\
  h_{(i)}(k)
  &:=
  E_{i}(k)
  +
  \varepsilon
  b
  \cdot
  \mathrm{Im}
  \left(
    \mathrm{tr}_{\mathbb{C}^{4}}
    \left(
      \tilde{P}_{\textrm{km}}^{\lbrace i \rbrace}(k)
      \partial_{1}
      \tilde{P}_{\textrm{km}}^{\lbrace i \rbrace}(k)
      \left(
        \tilde{H}_{\textrm{km}}(k)
        -
        E_{i}(k)
        \cdot
        1_{\mathbb{C}^{4 \times 4}}
      \right)
      \partial_{2}
      \tilde{P}_{\textrm{km}}^{\lbrace i \rbrace}(k)
    \right)
  \right)
\end{align*}
\phantom{proven}
\hfill
$\square$
\end{con}
\phantom{proven}
\hfill
$\square$
\end{exa}
